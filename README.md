# DAX-ESG-Volatility-Analysis
Project Report: ESG Integration as a Volatility Buffer in DAX 20 Equities1. The ObjectiveAmidst increasing regulatory pressure from the EU Taxonomy and the German Supply Chain Act (LKSG), this study investigates the correlation between non-financial ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) performance and market risk. The goal was to determine if high ESG quality scores act as a "volatility buffer" for 20 major DAX companies during the post-pandemic market recovery period (2021–2024).2. MethodologyThe analysis was performed using R (v4.3). Financial time-series data was ingested via the quantmod API, pulling adjusted closing prices for 20 DAX-listed tickers.Risk Metric: Annualized volatility was calculated as the standard deviation of daily log returns ($\sigma \cdot \sqrt{252}$).Sustainability Metric: MSCI-estimated ESG Quality Scores were utilized as the independent variable.Statistical Model: An Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Regression was applied to quantify the sensitivity of market risk to sustainability ratings.3. The Core FindingThe empirical results support the "Risk-Mitigation Hypothesis." The regression model identified a negative coefficient of -0.0142.Quantitative Insight: For every 1-point increase in a company’s ESG Quality Score, annualized volatility decreased by 1.42%.While the R-squared value (8.4%) indicates that ESG is one of many factors driving price stability, the consistent downward slope of the regression line suggests that sustainability is a material indicator of idiosyncratic risk.4. The SAP/Finance Bridge: Strategic ImplicationsFrom a Corporate Governance and SAP Finance perspective, these results underscore the importance of integrating non-financial KPIs into traditional risk frameworks. High ESG scores are not merely "ethical" badges; they are proxies for:Operational Resilience: Superior internal controls that reduce the likelihood of "Tail Risks" such as environmental litigation or labor scandals.Cost of Capital: Markets reward transparent governance with lower volatility, potentially leading to better credit ratings and lower borrowing costs.Regulatory Future-Proofing: Companies already aligned with high 'G' (Governance) standards are better positioned to absorb the costs of upcoming ESG disclosures without the market shocks seen in lower-rated peers
